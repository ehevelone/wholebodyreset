Pause & Support

This page appears when the body is signaling that it needs more time, less pressure, or gentler pacing. This is a normal and expected part of terrain-based work.

Rather than pushing forward, the goal here is to listen. The body often communicates through subtle changes before more intense symptoms appear.

Common signals that a pause may be helpful include:

  • Increased or persistent fatigue
  • Anxiety, restlessness, or nervous system agitation
  • Digestive slowing, constipation, or loss of appetite
  • Lymph congestion, pressure, or a heavy feeling
  • Sleep disruption or feeling unrefreshed after rest

These signals are not failure. They are information. They indicate that the current load may be exceeding the body’s available capacity.

What This Often Indicates

In many cases, the body is asking for one or more of the following:

  • Reduced stimulation or fewer inputs
  • Slower pacing or longer spacing between changes
  • More time reinforcing foundational supports
  • Additional personalization or guidance if available

Pausing does not undo progress. In fact, it often preserves it. Giving the nervous system time to settle allows regulation to catch up with demand.

A pause is a form of support. It protects the nervous system, reduces cumulative stress, and helps prevent longer setbacks later.

When the body is ready, forward movement tends to feel steadier, clearer, and less reactive.
