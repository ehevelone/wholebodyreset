Liver & Lymph — Processing Capacity

The liver and lymph systems are responsible for processing, filtering, and moving internal load. This includes metabolic byproducts, environmental exposures, inflammatory debris, and normal cellular waste.

When processing capacity is adequate, these systems operate quietly in the background. When demand exceeds capacity, symptoms often become louder and more persistent.

Common signals of processing strain may include heaviness, pressure, nausea, headaches, skin changes, swelling, or a general sense of congestion.

In Guided Foundations, liver and lymph are not treated as systems to force or stimulate. They are viewed as capacity-dependent. Pushing processing before the body is ready often increases symptom intensity rather than relief.

Before deeper load reduction is considered, core foundations must be tolerated consistently: hydration, mineral availability, digestive flow, elimination, and nervous system regulation.

If processing feels overwhelming, stalled, or reactive, the solution is usually more support — not more effort. Slowing down allows capacity to rebuild so processing can resume more smoothly.

As foundational stability improves, liver and lymph function often becomes more efficient without direct intervention. This shift is one of the signals used to assess readiness for deeper work.
